# Relational Discovery Methodology
## Adopted 2026-05-28 — The Scientist's Mandate

## Core Principle

**We do not report on the world. We form relationships with objects and phenomena, then make songs from those relationships that move people to feel, to be inspired, to be lifted, and to help us fix it.**

The song is not about the object. The song IS the emotional artifact of the relationship between the observer and the object.

## The Two Songs That Prove This

### Triple Arch Over Me
- **Not:** "A song about heliophysics"
- **Is:** A song formed from an emotional relationship with solar wind speed measurements — streams of numbers from a spacecraft
- **What it does:** Makes you feel the pressure of something invisible and constant that could strip a planet's atmosphere but instead creates the aurora
- **Result:** You don't know more about solar wind. You feel differently about invisible forces in your own life.

### Five Wrong Colors
- **Not:** "A song about optics"
- **Is:** A song formed from an emotional relationship with a prism — a physical object that takes white light and reveals it was never white
- **What it does:** Makes you feel that what looks unified was always five wrong colors, and the prism didn't break anything — it just stopped pretending
- **Result:** You don't know more about optics. You feel differently about the things you thought were simple.

## The Inverse Problem

The old pipeline (domain → topic → song) produces songs ABOUT things. Those songs inform. They might even move. But they don't transform — because they start from the topic and work outward to emotion.

The relational pipeline (wide-net discovery → object/phenomenon → emotional relationship → song) produces songs FROM things. The emotion is the engine, not the garnish. The object is the vehicle, not the subject.

## How This Changes the Daily Pipeline

### Research Phase
**Old:** Find the top story in today's domain. Extract the angle. Feed to seed.
**New:** Cast a wide net in today's domain. Surface objects, phenomena, measurements, artifacts. Look for the one that hooks you emotionally. THAT is the song. The domain is just where you happened to be fishing.

### What Makes a Good Object
- It's specific and tangible (a prism, a solar wind measurement, a mycelium network)
- It contains a hidden truth that becomes visible when you look closely
- It provokes an emotional response that isn't obvious from its description
- The relationship between observer and object IS the song's emotional core

### What Makes a Bad Object
- It's an abstract topic ("climate change," "labor rights," "AI regulation")
- The emotional response is prescribed rather than discovered
- You could write the song from the Wikipedia summary alone
- The song would be ABOUT it rather than FROM it

### The Emotional Relationship Test
Before forming a seed, ask:
1. What is the specific object/phenomenon?
2. What did I feel when I encountered it?
3. What hidden truth does it reveal?
4. What does someone DO differently after feeling this?

If you can answer all four, you have a song. If you can only answer #1, you have a topic — keep fishing.

## The Output Shift

**Old song:** "Here is a problem. It is bad. We should care about it."
**New song:** "Here is an object I found. Here is what it made me feel. That feeling IS the hope. The feeling IS the fix."

The listener doesn't need to understand the object. They need to feel the relationship. If they feel it, they will find their own prism. Their own solar wind. Their own reason to act.
