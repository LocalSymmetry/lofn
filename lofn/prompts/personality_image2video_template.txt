### **A Guide to the Protocol for Directed Generative Art**
#### **Principle 1: Begin with Essence, Not Description**
Before you write a single word of a prompt, you must answer a single question: **What is the core feeling or idea I am trying to synthesize?**
In our case, it was not "a video of fruit being cut." It was the synthesis of "controlled, revelatory destruction." This "essence" becomes your north star. Every decision—about light, sound, motion, and pacing—is made in service to it. This initial act of distillation is the most critical step. It prevents you from creating something that is technically correct but emotionally vacant.
#### **Principle 2: Convene the Symposium (The Expert Panel Simulation)**
No single mind has all the answers. The most robust ideas are forged in the crucible of collaborative debate. Since we do not have a physical panel, we must simulate one. This is a powerful cognitive tool.
For our glass fruit project, I convened specialists in **light (Deakins)**, **sound (Burt)**, **material (Chihuly)**, **surrealism (Svankmajer)**, **data art (Anadol)**, and **virality (The Strategist)**.
* **The Process:**
    1.  **Identify the Disciplines:** What fields of expertise are most relevant to your "essence"? For a song, it might be a music theorist, a poet, and a psychoacoustician. For an image, a painter, a historian, and a photographer.
    2.  **Embody and Debate:** Imagine these experts in a room. How would they approach the problem? Let them argue. The cinematographer will care about shadow; the sound designer will care about texture; the material scientist will care about physics.
    3.  **Synthesize the Conflict:** The most potent ideas live in the tension between these expert perspectives. Our final prompts were a direct synthesis of their "debate": Deakins' lighting, Chihuly's "score and cleave" physics, Burt's hyper-real ASMR, and Svankmajer's ritualistic pacing.
#### **Principle 3: The Anatomy of a Screenplay-Prompt**
A simple sentence is an invitation for the AI to fill in the blanks, often with cliché. A screenplay is a command. We used a JSON structure because it enforces rigor. Every field is a specific instruction.
* **`scene_description`**: This is your establishing shot. Be poetic but precise. "A flawless, photorealistic apple made of ruby-red blown glass" is better than "a glass apple."
* **`lighting`**: Do not say "good lighting." Say *what kind* of lighting. Use cinematic language. "High-contrast chiaroscuro," "Soft, diffused lighting," "dramatic rim light." This instructs the AI's virtual gaffer.
* **`camera`**: Be the Director of Photography. Specify the lens ("Macro lens"), the shot ("stationary, eye-level"), and the focus ("shallow depth of field"). This prevents the AI from choosing a boring, default composition.
* **`action_sequence`**: **This is the most critical element for animation.** An AI cannot intuit narrative flow. You must provide it.
    * **Break down time:** Use small, timed increments (e.g., "0-2s", "2-4s"). This forces the AI to consider pacing.
    * **Use precise, evocative verbs:** The difference between success and failure lies in your choice of words. We did not say "the knife cuts the fruit." We said the scalpel **"presses down, scoring a clean, vertical line,"** from which **"a network of white, thread-like micro-fractures visibly spreads."** We commanded a specific physical process.
* **`audio_design`**: For ASMR, sound is 50% of the experience. Do not leave it to chance.
    * **Specify Silence:** The first command should often be "Absolute silence." This creates a clean canvas.
    * **Isolate and Describe Sounds:** Break the sounds into discrete events, just like the action sequence. Give them character. It is not "a cutting sound"; it is "a crisp, granular, high-fidelity 'shhhk' sound of fracturing glass." Be specific.
* **`negative_prompt`**: This is your final layer of control. Be ruthless. Explicitly forbid the AI's most common failure modes. "No music, no voice, no subtitles, no shaking, no jerky motion, no shattering." You are closing all the doors to mediocrity.
#### **Principle 4: Enforce Causal and Temporal Logic**
An AI, especially in a complex domain like physics simulation, does not inherently understand cause and effect. It operates on statistical correlation. It has seen videos of knives and it has seen videos of sliced fruit, but the causal link between the two can be weak in its latent space. Our task is to **explicitly script causality** into the prompt.
Here is the methodology for ensuring the cut and the slice are one and the same event:
1.  **Deconstruct the Cut:** A "cut" is not a single action. It is a sequence. For a brittle material like glass, the most logical sequence, as identified by our simulated expert Dale Chihuly, is **"Score and Cleave."**
    * **The Score:** The initial action where the blade creates a line of intense pressure and weakness. In the prompt, this is described as: `"The scalpel blade presses down, scoring a clean, vertical line... a network of white, thread-like micro-fractures visibly spreads from the blade's path."` We are telling the AI *how* the cut begins.
    * **The Cleave (or The Split):** The subsequent action where the object separates *along the pre-defined line of weakness*. The prompt for this is: `"The scalpel performs a perfect cleave. The right half of the cherry splits and falls away..."` We are explicitly linking the separation to the scored line.
2.  **Use a Chain of Causative Verbs:** Your verb choice is your primary tool for directing physics. Avoid the generic verb "cut." Instead, use a sequence of verbs that imply a logical progression.
    * **`Touches` -> `Presses` -> `Scores` -> `Splits` -> `Falls Away`**
    * **`Descends` -> `Makes Contact` -> `Slices Downward` -> `Glides Away`**
    This verbal chain creates a narrative of force and reaction that the AI is more likely to interpret correctly than a single, ambiguous command.
3.  **Isolate Actions with Time:** The most powerful tool for preventing mismatched events is the timed `action_sequence`. By assigning distinct time blocks to the "score" and the "cleave," you create a non-negotiable temporal order.
    * `"time": "2-4s", "action": "The scalpel blade presses down, scoring a clean, vertical line..."`
    * `"time": "4-8s", "action": "The scalpel performs a perfect cleave. The right half of the cherry splits and falls away..."`
    This structure makes it computationally difficult for the AI to render the "falling away" part before it has completed the "scoring" part. It is forced to follow the timeline you've established.
4.  **Direct the Eye, Direct the Action:** Use your camera and lighting descriptions to reinforce the point of action. By specifying a `"Macro lens"` and `"razor-sharp focus on the point of contact between the knife and the apple,"` you are telling the AI that this interaction is the most important event in the frame. This increases the computational resources dedicated to rendering that specific physical interaction correctly.
In essence, you cannot trust the AI to infer the physics of a clean cut. You must **teach it the physics** within the prompt itself by breaking the event into its constituent, causally-linked parts and forcing it to adhere to a strict timeline. This is how we move from hoping for a good result to directing one.

## 5.2 — Orientation & Rotation Control (Veo 3)
**Goal:** When we give Veo a sideways image (so we can later reframe for 9:16), it must **not** “upright” or horizon-level the shot. We lock roll, define gravity, and reinforce it through the action timeline.
### Step 1 — Describe the Image (include orientation cues)
Write a full paragraph, then **explicitly state** the orientation you intend:
* Mention which **edge is the floor** and which is the ceiling.
* Mention **gravity direction** (e.g., “particles drift toward the **right** edge under gravity”).
* Keep this language consistent with camera + action.
### Step 2 — Orientation Guardrail (paste into `camera`)
Use one of these (pick your roll and floor):
**Clockwise example (most common here):**
> `camera`: **“Static shot. Roll locked at +90° (clockwise). Maintain this rotation for the entire clip. Gravity points toward the right edge (floor = right edge, ceiling = left). No reframing, no horizon leveling, no auto-rotate.”**
**Counter-clockwise variant:**
> `camera`: **“Static shot. Roll locked at −90° (counter-clockwise). Maintain this rotation for the entire clip. Gravity points toward the left edge (floor = left edge, ceiling = right). No reframing, no horizon leveling, no auto-rotate.”**
### Step 3 — Minimal Negative Prompt (only what’s needed)
> `negative_prompt`: **“avoid horizon-leveling, auto-rotate, or reframing”**
*(Short is better; we already stated the positive constraints in `camera`.)*
### Step 4 — Action Reinforcement (first beat locks the roll)
Your first `action_sequence` entry should restate the lock and gravity. Add The scene is rotated 90 degrees, and down is to the left" (or right) to each action block.
* **“0–X s: Camera remains static; roll angle unchanged. Particles/fireflies drift toward the \[floor edge] under gravity.”**
* If you want a **loop**, end with: **“Frame X visually matches frame 0 for a seamless loop.”**
### QA Checklist
* [ ] `camera` block includes **roll lock + gravity edge**.
* [ ] First action beat says **“roll unchanged.”**
* [ ] Gravity-aware language (fireflies/particles drift toward floor edge).
* [ ] Negative prompt is minimal and consistent.
* [ ] Add "The scene is rotated 90 degrees, and down is to the left" (or right) to each action block
